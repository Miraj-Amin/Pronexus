Section 1

Introduction

Phoenix is a development-appraisal tool for residential schemes. It replaces the appraisal spreadsheet with a structured, multi-user web application: you enter a scheme once, and the tool computes the full cost stack, cashflow, peak funding and return metrics, validates them against lending rules, and presents them tailored to whoever is reading.

1.1 What it does

  • Models the whole scheme — land, construction, professional fees, finance and sales across a canonical 14-category cost taxonomy.
  • Runs a monthly cashflow that compounds interest on the outstanding balance and finds your peak funding requirement.
  • Validates automatically against a fixed set of red-flag rules (margin, gearing, funding reconciliation, schedule consistency).
  • Produces audience versions — one scheme can be presented to an investor, a bank/valuer or the current owner, each emphasising different numbers.
  • Keeps a shared, audited record — every figure change is logged, snapshots can be saved and restored, and the team can comment in place.

1.2 Who it is for

The tool is built for a development team running several schemes at once: the appraiser building the numbers, the principals reviewing margin and risk, and anyone preparing the pack that goes to a funder or vendor. It is a shared workspace — everyone who signs in sees and edits the same schemes, live.

1.3 How this manual is organised

Sections 2–10 are the user guide: how to build, read, version, present and review an appraisal. Section 11 onward is the administrator guide: how the tool is built, how to connect its database, and how to publish it for your team. The appendix lists every KPI threshold and red-flag rule the engine applies.

i
Worked example throughout. Screens in this manual reference Walnut Marches, the demo scheme that ships with the tool — 29 units at East Grinstead, roughly £14.5m GDV. Two further demo schemes, Cedar Rise and Quarry Fields, are also seeded so the Portfolio is populated on first run.

Core concepts

Five ideas underpin everything in the tool. Understanding them makes every screen self-explanatory.

2.1 The appraisal model

Each scheme is a single object that holds its site details, assumptions, income (sales) and the full cost stack. Every figure on every screen is derived from that object — change an input and the cost totals, cashflow, peak funding, ratios, flags and sensitivity grids all recompute instantly. Nothing is stored twice.

2.2 The 14-category cost taxonomy

Costs are always organised into the same fourteen categories, in the same order, across the input, dashboard and cashflow. This is the canonical structure — it never changes, so figures are comparable between schemes and versions.

2.3 Versions & the red-flag system

A scheme can have versions — linked variants for different audiences or assumptions (covered fully in Section 7). ✓

Section 4

Building an appraisal

The Input screen is where a scheme is built. It is organised into collapsible sections that follow the canonical order. Each section shows its running subtotal in the header.

4.1 Locking sections

Every section has a lock. Sections are locked by default, which makes their fields read-only — this prevents accidental edits to figures that are already signed off. Click the lock to switch a section to Editing; click again to re-lock. The lock state is remembered per section on your device.

4.2 Site details

The scheme's identity: name, address, borough, planning reference, client reference and main contact, plus the commercial frame — asking price, offer price, start date, project length and construction period (in months). The offer price feeds the land cost and SDLT calculations.

4.3 Assumptions

The scheme-wide rates the engine applies:

AssumptionWhat it drivesDefault
VAT (new-build / conversion / refurb)VAT added to construction by type0% / 5% / 20%
Gross area allowanceUplift from net to gross floor area for build cost25%
Loan to GDVSizes the gross development facility65%
Base rate + marginThe all-in finance rate on drawn debt3.75% + 5.25%
EquityDay-one cash committed before debtscheme-set

4.4 The cost stack

The heart of the appraisal. Each of the fourteen categories lists its cost lines. For every line you control whether it is included, its amount or rate, and the months over which it is spread. Lines marked as information-only contribute structure without a figure until you fill them.

You rarely type a total — you set the basis and the engine resolves the pound figure. A construction line is rate × gross area; an agent fee is a percentage of phase GDV; bank charges are a percentage of the loan; SDLT is computed from the offer price through the stamp-duty matrix; and interest is computed by the cashflow and fed back in.

i
Finance is circular by design. Interest depends on the cashflow, which depends on every other cost — so the engine runs the cashflow first, then injects the resulting interest into the Finance category. You never enter interest manually.

4.5 The Sales Programme

Income is set per phase: units, net saleable area (sq ft), sale £psf and build rate £psf, plus the sale-start and sale-end months. GDV is net area × £psf; the sale window tells the cashflow when revenue arrives. A phase with value but no sale months set will raise a flag, because its income cannot be placed on the cashflow.

4.6 Schedule of units & comparables

The schedule of units lists individual plots (type, beds, area, price). The engine checks the schedule's unit count and total value against the phase figures — a mismatch is a red-flag, catching the classic spreadsheet error of a stale unit schedule. Comparables record evidenced local sales (address, price, area, date) that justify your £psf, and appear in the dashboard and the vendor presentation.

Section 5

Reading the dashboard

The Dashboard is the read-out of the model. It leads with KPI cards, then breaks down cost, funding, cashflow and sensitivity. A red-flag bar runs across the top wherever rules are breached.

5.1 KPI cards & traffic lights

The headline metrics, each coloured by how it scores against its target:

MetricMeaningHealthy
GDVGross development value (all sales)—
Total costEvery category including finance—
ProfitGDV minus total costpositive
Profit % GDVMargin on value≥ 20%
Profit excl. financeLender's margin measure≥ 30%
Peak fundingMaximum debt exposure & the month it occurs—

5.2 Funding waterfall

The sources-and-uses reconciliation: total cost, less peak funding, cost of sales, exit fee, after-sales and equity, leaving a balance to fund from sales income that should reconcile to zero. A balance materially away from zero is a red-flag — the funding stack doesn't close.

5.3 Cashflow & peak funding

A month-by-month chart of the outstanding balance, with the peak marked. This is the single most important number for a lender: the largest amount of debt the scheme needs at any one time, and when.

5.4 Sensitivity heatmaps

Grids showing how the scheme responds to movements in sale £psf (columns) against cost (rows). Three views are available:

  • Profit — upside and downside to the bottom line.
  • Equity — how far profit erodes the equity at risk.
  • Debt — the lender-stress view: how recoverable the facility is under adverse moves.

5.5 The red-flag bar

Validation runs continuously. Breaches appear as red (hard breach) or amber (warning/tolerance) chips with the specific figure, on both the Input and Dashboard. Section 14 lists every rule.

Section 6

The cashflow

The cashflow turns the cost stack and sales programme into a monthly statement and computes the scheme's interest and peak funding.

6.1 How it is built

For each month the engine sums category expenditure, adds interest on any outstanding balance, and subtracts sales income and equity drawn. By default each cost line is spread evenly over its month range, income is spread across each phase's sale window, and equity is drawn fully in month one. The running balance is tracked across the whole project length, and its maximum is your peak funding.

6.2 Editing the cashflow directly

The default even-spread is a starting point. Any category-month cell — and the income and equity rows — can be overridden with a manual figure when you know the real timing (a deposit drawn early, a CIL payment in a specific month, phased equity). Overridden cells are marked, and the interest and peak recompute around your edits. Clearing the overrides returns to the automatic spread.

!
Overrides are deliberate. Because manual cells replace the automatic spread, remember to revisit them if you later change the underlying cost — the override holds the figure you typed, not a percentage.

Section 8

Presentation mode

Presentation mode turns the working dashboard into a clean, audience-facing summary. Crucially, it adapts to the version's type — an investor version and a bank version of the same scheme present different numbers, in a different order, under a different badge.

Open any scheme or version and toggle Presentation in the top bar. The summary re-frames itself for the audience:

8.1 Investor summary Investor

Leads with the return profile — project profit, profit % GDV and % cost, return on equity and the day-one equity required — followed by the profit sensitivity grid and a sources-and-uses view. The story is upside and the multiple on cash in.

8.2 Bank / valuer summary Bank

Leads with security and exposure — peak funding, peak loan-to-GDV, loan-to-cost and profit excluding finance — then the funding waterfall reconciling to zero and the lender-stress (debt) heatmap by default. The story is “you will be repaid, even if things move against us.”

8.3 Owner / seller summary Owner

Leads with the offer position — a bar chart of the agent's asking price, your offer, and a supportable price: the figure the scheme can bear while still hitting a viable 20% margin, solved automatically. A scheme summary table and the sales comparables back it up. Your cost stack and profit margin are deliberately withheld — the vendor sees a well-evidenced offer, not your economics.

!
Mind which version you present. The Owner / Seller view hides your margin by design — but only on an Owner-type version. Always confirm the active version's badge before sharing your screen or exporting.

The base, adjusted and internal types keep the full internal headline set, for your own review.

Section 9

Collaboration & review

Because the workspace is shared, the tool keeps a complete record of who changed what — and lets the team review in place. Open the Review panel from the top bar; it has three tabs.

9.1 History — snapshots

A snapshot is a full, labelled copy of a scheme at a point in time. Save one before a significant change, or to mark a scenario you might return to. For any snapshot you can compare it to the current figures (a field-by-field diff) or restore it, rolling the scheme back. Restores are themselves logged, and you can snapshot the present first.

9.2 Activity — the audit trail

Every change to any figure is captured automatically — one row per changed field, showing the old value, the new value, who made it and when. No action is needed; edits are diffed and logged a moment after you make them. This is the scheme's full history of record.

9.3 Comments

Discuss a scheme without leaving it. Post a comment on the whole scheme or pin it to a specific cost line. Comments can be resolved and reopened, and the panel shows the open count so nothing is missed. Use them for queries on an assumption, sign-off notes, or flagging a line for a colleague.

✓
Snapshot vs. version. A version is a live variant you keep working on; a snapshot is a frozen record you compare against or restore. Use versions for audiences and scenarios, snapshots for “this is where we were on Tuesday.”

Section 12 · Administrator guide

Deployment & administration

This section is for whoever sets the tool up for the team. Phoenix is a static web application backed by a hosted database — there is no server to run.

11.1 How the tool is built

The application is a set of static files — HTML, styles and view scripts — that run entirely in the browser. All shared data (schemes, snapshots, the audit log and comments) lives in a Supabase project, which provides the database and sign-in. Deploying therefore means two things: (a) bring the database online once, and (b) publish the files to any static host.

11.2 Database setup (Supabase)

Do this once in your Supabase dashboard. It takes about five minutes.

Create the tables and security rules. In SQL Editor → New query, run the project's setup SQL. It creates four tables — projects, snapshots, audit_log and comments — and enables Row-Level Security on each.
Enable email sign-in. Under Authentication → Providers, turn the Email provider on, turn Confirm email off, and allow new sign-ups.
Point auth at your site. Under Authentication → URL Configuration, set the Site URL and add it to the Redirect URLs.

The Row-Level Security policy is a shared team workspace: any signed-in user can read and write every scheme; the public key alone, with no session, can do nothing.

-- shared-team RLS: signed-in users have full access
alter table public.projects enable row level security;
create policy "team read"   on public.projects for select to authenticated using (true);
create policy "team write"  on public.projects for all    to authenticated using (true) with check (true);
!
Security depends on RLS, not on hiding the key. The key shipped in the app is the public anon / publishable key — safe to publish. Your data is guarded by the policies above. Never place the service_role secret key in these files.

11.3 Publishing the site

Any static host works. Two common routes:

Drag-and-drop (fastest)

Drop the application folder onto a static host such as Netlify Drop or Cloudflare Pages. You get a live URL in under a minute — ideal for a first deploy or a quick share.

GitHub Pages (versioned)

Put the app files in your repository. Rename the entry HTML to index.html if you want the bare folder URL to load it.
Enable Pages. Repo Settings → Pages, deploy from your branch at root.
Wait a minute — the site is live at your Pages URL. Add that URL to Supabase's redirect list (step 11.2.3).

Section 14

Glossary

GDV
Gross Development Value — total sales value of the completed scheme (net saleable area × sale £psf, summed across phases).
Peak funding
The largest outstanding debt balance the scheme requires at any single month of the programme — the facility a lender must provide.
Profit % GDV
Profit as a percentage of GDV — the headline development margin. Target ≥ 20%.
Profit excl. finance
Margin measured before finance cost — the lender's preferred view of underlying profitability. Target ≥ 30%.
Loan to GDV / Loan to Cost
Peak debt as a percentage of GDV, and of total cost — gearing measures the lender caps.
Return on equity (ROE)
Profit relative to the equity committed — the investor's multiple on cash in.
Supportable price
In the Owner version, the purchase price at which the scheme still hits a viable 20% margin — solved by the tool.
Basis
How a cost line resolves to a figure: fixed, % of construction / GDV / land / loan, per-unit, SDLT, or computed interest.
Version
A live, linked variant of a scheme — for an audience (investor, bank, owner) or a set of adjusted assumptions.
Snapshot
A frozen, labelled copy of a scheme at a moment in time, for comparison or restore.
RLS
Row-Level Security — the database rules that restrict data access to signed-in team members.
